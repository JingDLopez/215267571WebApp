Ei: A Tribute to our Beloved Sister

This page is dedicated to my sister Ei, a true embodiment of excellence and compassion. Through her boundless compassion and unwavering love, she has touched our lives in countless ways, enriching our days with her kindness and grace.

  • Early Years
    My sister is the second child in the family, born 4 years after our eldest brother. I call her "Ate Ei" (Older Sister Ei in English). When Ate Ei entered elementary school at our hometown, she was considered a genius. She is the validectorian on every year level she went on, bringing pride and honor to the family, which continued until high school. She always bagged the top ranking at school, the Editor-in-Chief of the school newspaper, and the representative of the school on most intellectual competitions.

  • College Life
    When I was born and gained consicousness, she had already went to college. Thankfully, she doesn't have to rent alone since she is with my dad, who works as a taxi driver in the city. Our parents' savings are just enough to keep us by on our daily needs so my sister had to apply to multiple scholarships. She is also a consistent Dean's Lister (which awards them with tuition discounts), and also works summer jobs during vacation. She graduated top 1 in the whole college of accountancy in the university (UC), achieving yet another milestone.

  • 1941 - Tries to enroll in the military after the Pearl Harbor attack, but is rejected. Instead, the military asked his lab work on waterproof glue, DDT to control malaria, disinfectants, and other applied science.
  • 1942 - Receives a Ph.D. in Genetics and Plant Pathology
  • 1944 - Rejects a 100% salary increase from Dupont, leaves behind his pregnant wife, and flies to Mexico to head a new plant pathology program. Over the next 16 years, his team breeds 6,000 different strains of disease resistent wheat - including different varieties for each major climate on Earth.
  • 1953 - Crosses a short, sturdy dwarf breed of wheat with a high-yeidling American breed, creating a strain that responds well to fertilizer. It goes on to provide 95% of Mexico's wheat
